Why Study Design Is the First Decision You Make

Design determines what a study can and cannot tell you. No amount of statistical sophistication applied afterwards can fix a mismatched design — which is why it is decided before a single participant is enrolled.

Section 01 · Foundations

The Big Picture: Observational vs Experimental

Every design in epidemiology falls into one of two families, defined by a single question: who decided who got the exposure?

OBS

Observational — we watch

The researcher does not assign exposure. Nature, behaviour or circumstance has already done so — we simply measure and compare.

DescriptiveCase report · case series · ecological · cross-sectional
AnalyticalCohort · case-control
No random allocation → confounding is the central threat to validity.
EXP

Experimental — we assign

The researcher actively assigns the exposure or intervention — ideally at random — to balance known and unknown confounders.

Individual RCTParticipants randomised one by one
Cluster RCTWhole communities or clinics randomised
Randomisation → the strongest available evidence for cause and effect.
Section 02 · Descriptive Designs

Descriptive Studies: Telling the First Story

Descriptive studies describe disease by person, place and time. There is no comparison group — they generate hypotheses rather than test them.

Case report

A single unusual or instructive patient, described in clinical detail — often the first signal that something new is happening.

NEWLANDS CLINIC, HARARE, ZIMBABWE

Chimbetete, Chirimuta, Pascoe & Keiser (2019, South African Journal of HIV Medicine) reported the first documented four-class drug-resistant — effectively "untreatable" — HIV case in Zimbabwe: a 17-year-old who had been vertically infected and was failing third-line antiretroviral therapy.

Why a case report: this was a sentinel event with implications for the whole national programme, not something you would wait to study with a control group.

Case series / outbreak report

Describes a cluster of cases by person, place and time — usually the first organised response to a disease outbreak.

NATIONAL CHOLERA OUTBREAK, ZIMBABWE

Mashe, Chaibva, Nair et al. (2023, BMJ Open) described Zimbabwe's 2018–19 cholera outbreak using routine surveillance data: 10,730 suspected cases, an attack rate of 174.6 per 100,000, and a multidrug-resistant Vibrio cholerae O1 Ogawa strain confirmed by the National Microbiology Reference Laboratory.

Why a case series: outbreak response needs an immediate picture of who, where and when — formal comparison groups come later, if at all.

Ecological study

Exposure and outcome are measured at the group or area level, not the individual — efficient, but at risk of the "ecological fallacy" (a group-level association may not hold for individuals).

SPATIAL CHOLERA ANALYSIS, HARARE

Luque-Fernández, Mason, Gray et al. (2011, Transactions of the Royal Society of Tropical Medicine and Hygiene) used a secondary-data spatial analysis to link suburb-level water-supply interruptions to cholera risk during Harare's devastating 2008–09 epidemic.

Why ecological: water-supply records existed at suburb level, not per household — the analysis had to work at the level the data allowed.

Strength: fast, cheap, hypothesis-generating, vital for outbreak response.   Limitation: no comparison group — cannot test a causal hypothesis.

Section 02 · Descriptive Designs

Cross-Sectional Studies: A Snapshot in Time

Exposure and outcome are measured simultaneously, in a defined population, at one point in time — the design of choice for estimating prevalence.

ZIMPHIA — ZIMBABWE POPULATION-BASED HIV IMPACT ASSESSMENT

Gonese, Musuka, Ruangtragool et al. (2020, AIDS Research and Human Retroviruses) report on ZIMPHIA, a nationally representative household survey. In the 2015–16 round, 20,577 adults aged 15–64 completed interviews and HIV testing in a single visit — yielding prevalence (13.35% among those aged 15–49), viral-load suppression and progress toward the UNAIDS 90-90-90 targets, all from one snapshot.

13.4%

12.9%

Two independent cross-sectional rounds — not the same people followed over time — used to monitor the national HIV response.

Why cross-sectional: the goal was a population-wide estimate right now, not a causal story about any one person's HIV trajectory.

Strength: efficient for prevalence and health-system benchmarking; repeatable rounds reveal trends.   Limitation: cannot establish temporal sequence — which came first, the exposure or the outcome?

Section 03 · Analytical Observational Designs

Case-Control Studies: Working Backwards from the Outcome

Case-control studies start with the outcome and look backward in time for the exposure — the mirror image of a cohort study.

PAST
Exposure history
NOW
Cases (outcome+) & controls (outcome−)

PMTCT case-control, Chitungwiza Hospital, Zimbabwe

Ngwende, Gombe, Midzi, Tshimanga, Shambira & Chadambuka (2013, BMC Public Health) ran a 1:1 unmatched case-control study at Chitungwiza Hospital. Cases were HIV-positive infants and controls were HIV-negative infants, both born to mothers enrolled in the PMTCT programme.

Finding: maternal CD4 <200 cells/µL and mixed feeding raised infant HIV risk; exclusive breastfeeding for under six months was protective.

rVSV-ZEBOV vaccine effectiveness, Democratic Republic of the Congo

Meakin et al. (2024, Lancet Infectious Diseases) ran a retrospective test-negative study during the DRC's 2018–20 Ebola outbreak — a case-control variant in which "cases" are people who test positive for the disease in question and "controls" are people tested for the same illness who test negative, all drawn from the same treatment centres.

Finding: rVSV-ZEBOV vaccine effectiveness was 84% (95% credible interval 70–92) from 10 days after vaccination — the first real-world effectiveness estimate for this vaccine.

Central design challenge: choosing controls who represent the same source population as the cases — without the outcome — to avoid selection and recall bias.

Section 03 · Analytical Observational Designs

Cohort Studies: Following Forward Through Time

Cohort studies start with exposure status and follow participants forward to see who develops the outcome.

BASE
Exposed / unexposed, outcome-free
FU
Follow-up over time
OUT
Compare incidence of outcome
THE TENART COHORT, NEWLANDS CLINIC, HARARE

Shamu, Chimbetete, Shawarira-Bote, Mudzviti & Luthy (2017, PLoS ONE) followed a prospective HIV cohort at Newlands Clinic, Harare, forward through a decade of comprehensive antiretroviral care — tracking mortality, retention and virologic outcomes among Zimbabwean adults on ART. The cohort has continued generating forward-following evidence ever since, from third-line ART outcomes to cancer incidence among people living with HIV.

Why cohort: the question was about long-term outcomes of a treatment programme — that requires watching the same people over years, not a single snapshot.

PROSPECTIVE vs RETROSPECTIVE

Prospective: outcomes have not happened yet when enrolment begins. Retrospective: exposure and follow-up are reconstructed from existing records — still forward in logic, even though the data already exist.

Strength: establishes temporality; one exposure can be linked to many outcomes.

Limitation: costly, slow, and prone to loss-to-follow-up bias — a major issue for long-running African ART cohorts.

Section 04 · Experimental Designs

Randomised Trials: Individual and Cluster

In an experimental study, the researcher actively assigns the exposure — ideally by chance — so that known and unknown confounders are balanced across groups without anyone choosing who gets what.

The PALM trial — Ebola therapeutics, Democratic Republic of the Congo

Mulangu, Dodd, Davey et al. for the PALM Consortium (2019, New England Journal of Medicine) randomised 681 patients with confirmed Ebola virus disease, during the DRC's 2018–20 North Kivu/Ituri outbreak, in a 1:1:1:1 ratio to ZMapp, remdesivir, mAb114 or REGN-EB3.

Finding: mAb114 and REGN-EB3 significantly reduced mortality versus ZMapp — the first randomised trial of Ebola therapeutics conducted during an active outbreak, run jointly by DRC and international investigators under an independent safety monitoring board.

HPTN 071 (PopART) — Zambia & South Africa, and DETECTB — Harare

HPTN 071 (PopART): Hayes et al. (2019, NEJM) randomised 21 communities (around 1 million people) in Zambia and South Africa to combination HIV prevention with universal test-and-treat, or to standard of care — measuring population-level HIV incidence.

DETECTB: Corbett et al. (2010, Lancet) randomised 46 neighbourhood clusters in Harare (HIV prevalence around 21%) to two active TB case-finding strategies; the mobile-van strategy found 1.48× more infectious TB cases than door-to-door visits.

Why cluster, not individual: the intervention (a community testing campaign, a van visiting a neighbourhood) naturally applies to a whole group — individually randomising neighbours would let the intervention "leak" across arms.

Analysis note: outcomes within a cluster are correlated — a naïve individual-level analysis overstates precision; methods must account for intra-cluster correlation.

Choosing the Right Design

Section 06 · Reading Critically

Cross-Cutting Pitfalls: Bias, Confounding & Generalisability

Every design carries its own typical bias — recognising it is half the work of critical appraisal.

Selection bias

Who ends up in the study — or stays in it — differs systematically from who should. Loss-to-follow-up in long-running ART cohorts is a classic example.

Information / recall bias

Exposure or outcome is measured or remembered differently between groups — a key risk when case-control studies ask mothers to recall infant feeding practices.

Confounding

A third factor (e.g. HIV status) distorts the exposure–outcome link — central to interpreting TB risk in HIV-prevalent Harare settings.

Generalisability

Will results from one outbreak setting (e.g. North Kivu, DRC) transfer to a different population, health system or epidemic?

Your weekly quiz and Final Presentation Project will ask you to name the design and spot the most likely bias in a published abstract — just like the examples above.
