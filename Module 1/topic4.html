What is Routine Data?

Routine data: Data collected systematically through ongoing health, administrative, and social service systems, NOT originally collected to answer a specific research question.

Why Use Routine Data?

Large populations providing national, regional, or clinic-wide coverage.
Readily available, accessible, and continuously compiled.
Highly cost-effective since data collection infrastructure already exists.
Enables rapid hypothesis testing and operational clinical auditing.
Continuous over time, allowing robust trend monitoring across decades.
Reflects real-world, unselected populations in daily clinical care.
3 / 18

Two Main Uses of Routine Data

Descriptive Studies

• Monitor disease burden and trends over time.
• Compare disease frequency by geography, age, and sex.
• Trace public health trajectories (increasing vs decreasing).
• Identify underlying healthcare inequities.
• Example: Mapping HIV viral suppression margins across Zimbabwean provinces.

Analytical Studies

• Investigate potential causes of disease and comorbidities.
• Compare health outcomes between exposed and unexposed groups.
• Estimate relative and attributable risks within cohorts.
• Execute rapid, database-driven hypothesis testing.
• Example: Assessing metabolic syndrome risk factors using longitudinal electronic clinic records.

Major Sources of Routine Data

5 / 18

Denominator Data: Census & Population

Population Census

National survey managed by ZIMSTAT counting total population by age, sex, location, and socio-economic characteristics.

Zimbabwe Update: The 2022 Census established a baseline population of ~15.1 million people. Intercensal projection models adjust these figures annually using localized birth, mortality, and migration dynamics.

Civil Registration & Vital Statistics (CRVS)

  • Birth registration: Establishes vital cohort denominators; captures parameters like birthweight and gestational age.
  • Death registration: Adjusts population denominators; provides mortality data across underlying and contributing causes.
⚠️ Limitation: Birth and death registration coverage can face significant under-reporting challenges in remote rural communities.

Numerator Data: Hospital Records

Hospital Data: Inpatient admissions, outpatient consultations, and discharge summaries from secondary, tertiary, and quaternary referral facilities.

Core Parameters Logged:

  • Admissions and discharges categorized by standardized ICD diagnostic codes.
  • Length of hospital stay and bed occupancy rates.
  • Surgical or medical procedures performed during admission.
  • Demographic arrays (age, sex, referring facility).
  • Discharge outcomes (discharged home, died in facility, transferred out).
Regional Application: Central referral complexes (e.g., Parirenyatwa or Mpilo Central Hospital) utilize automated summaries to monitor shifting admission burdens, such as rising cardiovascular admissions and persistent advanced HIV disease cases.
7 / 18

Numerator Data: Primary Care & Clinic Records

Clinic & Primary Health Care Data

Operational footprints generated dynamically across municipal clinics, polyclinics, rural health centres, and partner NGO facilities.

  • Patient demographics (age, biological sex, residential location).
  • Primary reason for presentation or clinical syndromic diagnosis.
  • Pharmaceutical prescriptions and immunisations delivered.
  • Downstream clinical referrals to district or provincial specialists.
The EMR Advantage: Specialized hubs, including the Newlands Clinic, maintain rich Electronic Medical Record (EMR) architectures. This enables seamless, longitudinal extraction of longitudinal CD4 trajectories, viral load patterns, drug adherence metrics, and non-communicable comorbidities.
8 / 18

Numerator Data: Disease Registries

Disease Registry: A centralized, systematic database tracking all identified cases of a specific disease entity within a defined population boundary.

Prominent Registries operating in Zimbabwe:

National TB Register: Tracks all notified Tuberculosis cases; logs diagnostic modes (GeneXpert), drug-resistance patterns, HIV co-infection status, and final treatment outcomes.
National Longitudinal ART Register: Tracks over 1.3 million people living with HIV on lifetime antiretroviral therapy, monitoring regimen switches and retention cascades.
Maternal Health Registries: Captures antenatal bookings, maternal complications, delivery interventions, and neonatal outcomes.
Zimbabwe National Cancer Registry (ZNCR): Compiles histopathological and clinical notifications to trace oncological trends, primarily across major urban hubs.
9 / 18

Zimbabwe Health Management Information System (HMIS)

The Platform (DHIS2): A web-based national architecture configured by the Ministry of Health and Child Care (MoHCC) to aggregate monthly health summaries from all reporting public, private, and NGO facilities.

Primary Aggregation Metrics in DHIS2:

Functional Category Core Programmatic Indicators Logged
Maternal & Neonatal ANC presentation weeks, institutional births, maternal deaths, PMTCT updates.
Child Health & EPI Vaccination coverage rates (BCG, MR), acute malnutrition screenings (MUAC).
Disease Surveillance Weekly statutory notifications for Malaria, Cholera, Typhoid, and outbreak-prone conditions.
HIV & TB Portfolios New ART initiations, viral load coverage, TB sputum alerts, co-infection treatment success.

Data Challenges: Facility data completeness can vary; reporting lags occur occasionally in rural environments with connectivity constraints.

Antenatal Clinic (ANC) Surveillance: A National Model

ANC-based HIV Surveillance: Utilises routine HIV testing data from pregnant women to generate reliable estimates of national and sub-national HIV prevalence trends over time.

Operational Steps:

  1. Every pregnant woman presenting at an ANC site undergoes routine opt-out HIV testing.
  2. Testing metrics are systematically logged within the standardized facility ANC register.
  3. Aggregated monthly summaries (Total tested, Total positive, Total negative) flow up to district levels.
  4. National-tier epidemiologists compile and stratify these prevalence data by province and age brackets.
Public Health Impact: Long-term ANC surveillance documented a substantial decline in national HIV prevalence from over 20% down to current stabilized margins around 11%, directly guiding localized resource allocation.

Data Linkage: Connecting Different Data Sources

Data Linkage: The methodology of connecting distinct data records from different tracking systems belonging to the same individual or institution to build a comprehensive clinical profile.

High-Yield Opportunities:

TB/HIV Register Synthesis: Linking the National TB Register with Electronic ART Systems reveals specific screening omissions, maps co-infection tracking, and measures therapeutic interactions.
Maternal-Child Linkage: Connecting maternal ANC cards with child immunisation logs provides long-term, real-world monitoring of PMTCT programme effectiveness.
Hospital-Community Synthesis: Linking tertiary discharge records with local primary clinic charts tracks re-admission rates and continuity of chronic care.
✓ Core Advantage: Generates rich, deep clinical insight without requiring costly primary fieldwork.
⚠️ Core Challenge: Demands robust matching tokens; requires rigorous ethical approvals and encryption safeguards.

Calculating Measures of Occurrence from Routine Data

Key Formula:

Measure of Occurrence = Numerator (Cases) / Denominator (Population at Risk)

Data Components Required:

Numerator

Events extracted from:
• Hospital discharge logs
• Electronic clinic records
• Specialized disease registries
• Civil death certificates

Denominator

Population values from:
• ZIMSTAT census projections
• Catchment tracking tools
• Aggregated ANC aggregates
• Active medical registry boundaries

Time Dimension

Boundaries from:
• Annual programmatic outputs
• Monthly DHIS2 summaries
• Quarterly cohort reporting windows
• Defined longitudinal follow-ups

Worked Example: Calculating Point Prevalence

Clinical Scenario: Evaluating metabolic comorbidity burdens within an active treatment cohort.

Data Parameters:

  • Numerator (Cases): Unique patients active on ART with a confirmed, documented diagnosis of Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus on June 30 = 160 cases.
  • Denominator (Population at Risk): Total unique adult patients living with HIV listed as actively enrolled at the facility on that exact date = 3,200 people.
  • Time Reference: Fixed cross-sectional point (30 June).

Calculation:

Point Prevalence = 160 / 3,200 = 0.05 = 5.0%

Clinical Interpretation: Exactly 5.0% of the active adult HIV cohort have a documented metabolic comorbidity. This point prevalence informs local pharmacy stock management and the design of integrated care protocols.

Strengths of Routine Data

Captures large, diverse populations, reducing selection biases found in tight trial environments.
Inexpensive and fast to access compared to launching primary cohort studies.
Maintains uninterrupted continuity, enabling long-term trend analysis across decades.
Provides granular clinical data, including lab values, vital signs, and drug regimens over time.
Allows for creative data linkage across separate databases, enriching research potential.
Minimises participant fatigue by safely utilising pre-existing administrative entries.
Highly sensitive for detecting rare clinical outcomes or localized disease clusters.
Generates practical, real-world evidence reflecting daily operational conditions.
15 / 18

Limitations of Routine Data

Variable Data Quality: Incomplete record fields, missing lab values, transcription errors, or shifting clinical case definitions.
Institutional Selection Bias: Restricts findings to individuals who actively access formal care, potentially missing wider community needs.
Unmeasured Confounding: Essential lifestyle, behavioral, and socio-economic variables are rarely captured in administrative logs.
Loss to Follow-up: Inability to trace true patient outcomes once an individual leaves a facility's geographical boundaries.
Privacy Risks: Linking disparate databases creates potential re-identification risks, demanding strict data governance.
Reporting Lags: Processing delays can make information outdated by the time it is compiled and accessible for analysis.

How to Interpret Routine Data: Critical Questions

  • Completeness: What proportion of expected facility submissions were actually received on time?
  • Diagnostic Accuracy: Are conditions confirmed through laboratory tests or subjective clinical assessments?
  • Denominator Validity: Is the population baseline current, or does it rely on outdated projections?
  • Timeliness: What is the time gap between initial field entry and final database finalisation?
  • Standardisation: Have case criteria remained uniform across the entire study period?
  • Confounding Controls: What crucial clinical or lifestyle variables are missing from this dataset?
  • Selection Bias: How does the care-seeking population differ from the general community?
  • Metadata Documentation: Are the underlying data dictionary and data collection methods clearly explained?

Key Takeaways

Routine Data: A powerful, cost-effective resource for generating actionable epidemiological evidence in resource-limited settings.
  • Zimbabwe maintains robust tracking infrastructure, including DHIS2, national HIV/TB logs, and EMR platforms.
  • Repurposing routine records can support both descriptive tracking and analytical cohort studies.
  • Secure data linkage can expand research potential without the expense of primary field data collection.
  • Always assess data quality, completeness, and potential selection biases before making clinical decisions.
  • Critical appraisal turns raw administrative records into reliable, real-world health evidence.
